<p>Ever seen someone ask an LLM, “Write me a blog post,” and then wonder why the output’s generic, off-brand, or just plain wrong?</p>

<p>That’s because <strong>AI doesn’t read minds</strong>—it follows instructions. Think of it like an <em>extremely high-IQ child</em>. Brilliant—but ask it vague questions, and they give you a blank stare.</p>

<p>There’s a classic peanut butter &amp; jelly sandwich experiment that illustrates this perfectly... and it’s brutal. Have you done it?</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Part 1: The Peanut Butter &amp; Jelly Sandwich Micdrop</strong></h4>

<p>Here’s the experiment in a nutshell:</p>

<p>Kids write down instructions like “Take bread, spread peanut butter, add jelly.” Dad (or AI) follows it <em>literally</em>, missing dozens of assumptions—like opening the jar, using the blade side, putting it on a plate. The result? A sandwich that’s chaos on a plate: plastic bread, butter-knife mishaps, mess everywhere .</p>

<p>That’s how LLMs operate. They execute your words—without your context. And without detail, you’re setting them up for failure.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Part 2: Why High-IQ Child Thinking Beats Prompting</strong></h4>

<p>Prompts are band-aids. They might get the job done <em>once</em>, but they don’t scale. You’re stuck recreating that perfect prompt over and over.</p>

<p>Treat AI like a bright child instead:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Give it <strong>structured context</strong> (brand documents, tone guides)</li>

<li>Define its <strong>role</strong> (“You’re a 10x growth marketer”)</li>

<li>Provide <strong>sequence and process</strong>—not just goals</li>
</ul>

<p>Then, it delivers—not guesses.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Part 3: What “Extremely High-IQ Child” Prompts Look Like</strong></h4>

<p>You wouldn’t tell a child “go clean your room” and expect perfection. Instead you'd say:</p>

<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>“Begin by gathering all clothes off the floor into a hamper.”</li>

<li>“Then sort them by color.”</li>

<li>“Fold and organize each pile.”</li>
</ol>

<p>Same with AI. Here’s how it translates:</p>

<p>Instead of: “Write my newsletter.”</p>

<p>You do:</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>“Here’s our brand voice.”</li>

<li>“Target reader: SaaS founders.”</li>

<li>“Objective: nurture leads into demo requests.”</li>

<li>“Follow this outline: hook, value prop, social proof, CTA.”</li>
</ul>

<p>That's it. Clarity unlocks performance.</p>

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Part 4: 5 Project-Based Structures You Must Build First</strong></h4>

<p>Before firing off prompts, build these:</p>

<p><strong>1. Context Upload Stack</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Brand docs, tone guides, past content</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>2. Role Definitions</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>“You are a fintech copywriter” or “a B2B SDR” </li>
</ul>

<p><strong>3. Step-by-Step Outlines</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Define sections: hook, insight, proof, CTA </li>
</ul>

<p><strong>4. Iteration &amp; Feedback System</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Ask for v1 → give edits → refine</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>5. Process Library</strong></p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Templates for blogs, emails, ads, reports</li>
</ul>

<p>These structures let AI <em>think</em> consistently. No guesswork.</p>
