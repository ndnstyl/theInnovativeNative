Background

Who I Am

I have spent nearly two decades inside growth systems that look functional on the surface and quietly fail underneath. My career has not been defined by channels, tools, or tactics. It has been defined by pattern recognition. I am usually brought in when leadership senses something is wrong but cannot articulate why, when performance appears busy but outcomes are decaying, and when teams are optimizing inside assumptions that no longer hold.

I am not interested in cosmetic improvement. I am interested in whether a system can survive contact with reality.

Over the last 20 years, I have worked across legal, professional services, SaaS, and high-constraint environments where the cost of being wrong compounds quickly. In those environments, belief-driven decision making is more dangerous than inaction. My role has consistently been to slow the system down, identify what is actually driving outcomes, preserve what compounds, and scrap what only looks productive.

What I Do in Plain English

Most teams do not need more effort. They need less guessing.

I come into systems that are already moving fast and ask a different question: what is actually causal here. When leadership cannot explain why results are changing, the system is no longer under control. At that point, adding budget, adding tools, or adding more people is usually how the failure gets more expensive.

I diagnose where the system is lying, where the reporting is comforting, and where the incentives are creating self-deception. Then I rebuild the system so performance is explainable, controllable, and durable.

How I Operate

Most organizations do not fail because nothing works. They fail because they misunderstand why something worked once. Legacy wins become emotional anchors. Teams protect familiar structures, double down on what feels safe, and quietly dismantle the very components producing signal.

I enter systems assuming three things:
	•	Some parts are working for reasons no one fully understands
	•	Some parts are being protected because they feel validating, not because they perform
	•	Most reporting tells a comforting story instead of a true one

My job is to identify which is which.

I make hard calls early. I am comfortable saying no to popular ideas, de-scoping vanity efforts, and naming when incremental optimization has become cosmetic. When re-architecture is required, I do it deliberately, with economic justification and operational clarity.

	•	Systems that do not collapse when attention moves elsewhere
