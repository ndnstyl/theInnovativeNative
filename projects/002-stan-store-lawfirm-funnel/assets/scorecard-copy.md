## Why This Scorecard Exists

Most law firms hemorrhage money through operational inefficiency. Not because the attorneys aren't good at law — but because the business operations around the law practice are still running like it's 1995.

I've spent the last three years building automation systems for service businesses. The pattern is consistent: firms doing $500K-$5M annually are leaving six figures on the table every year. Not through bad legal work. Through manual intake processes that lose leads. Through billable hours that never get captured. Through client communication that creates more work than it prevents.

This scorecard helps you identify where your firm sits on the automation spectrum. It's not a sales pitch. It's a diagnostic tool. Score yourself honestly across five operational areas. At the end, you'll know exactly where you're bleeding money — and what level of automation investment makes sense for your practice size.

### Who This Is For

- **Managing partners** in Bankruptcy, Criminal, or Administrative law firms doing $500K-$5M revenue
- **Solo practitioners** who've grown past the point where manual processes work
- **Firms that know they're inefficient** but don't know where to start fixing it

### How to Use This Scorecard

Each section below contains 5 assessment criteria. Score each section from 1-5 based on how accurately the description matches your current state:

- **1 = Completely manual / chaotic**
- **2 = Mostly manual with some tools**
- **3 = Partially automated but inconsistent**
- **4 = Mostly automated with some gaps**
- **5 = Fully automated and reliable**

Your total score will be out of 25. Be honest. The goal isn't to score high — it's to know where you actually are.

## Section 1: Client Intake & Onboarding

**Score this section 1-5 based on these criteria:**

### Assessment Questions

1. **How do new clients first contact you?**
   - **1:** Phone calls only, whoever answers writes notes on paper/email
   - **3:** We have a contact form, but follow-up is manual and inconsistent
   - **5:** Automated intake form that triggers conflict checks, engagement letters, and case file creation

2. **How long from initial contact to engagement letter signed?**
   - **1:** 5-7 days (lots of back-and-forth, information gets lost)
   - **3:** 2-3 days (we're faster but still manually coordinating)
   - **5:** Same day or next morning (automated workflows handle coordination)

3. **How do you collect practice-specific information?**
   - **1:** Phone intake or in-person meeting with manual note-taking
   - **3:** We send a PDF form they fill out and email back
   - **5:** Practice-specific digital forms that populate case management automatically

4. **What happens if a lead contacts you outside business hours?**
   - **1:** They wait until we see it, often losing them to faster competitors
   - **3:** Auto-reply email, but no actual progress until we're back
   - **5:** Full intake process can complete 24/7, conflict check runs automatically

5. **How do you handle conflict checks and retainer collection?**
   - **1:** Manual review of existing client lists, separate follow-up for retainer
   - **3:** Database search but manual coordination with accounting for payment
   - **5:** Automated conflict screening, integrated payment processing, trust accounting compliance

### Practice-Area Examples

**Bankruptcy:**
- Are you still collecting means test data via phone or paper? That's a 30-minute call that could be a 10-minute form.
- How long does it take to verify creditor lists and account numbers? Manual entry vs automated parsing saves 2-3 hours per case.

**Criminal:**
- When someone calls about charges, how quickly can you verify jurisdiction, check conflicts, and send an engagement letter? Speed wins these cases.
- Are you manually tracking court jurisdictions and judge assignments, or does your system flag conflicts automatically?

**Administrative Law:**
- When an agency denial comes in, how fast can you identify the agency, verify deadlines, confirm standing, and start the case file?
- Manual administrative filings are where most time gets lost — forms that could be templated are rebuilt from scratch.

## Section 2: Document & Case Management

**Score this section 1-5 based on these criteria:**

### Assessment Questions

1. **How are case files organized?**
   - **1:** Physical folders, inconsistent digital storage, documents hard to find
   - **3:** Shared drives with folder structure, but naming is inconsistent
   - **5:** Case management system with standardized filing, version control, and search

2. **How do you handle document generation (petitions, motions, pleadings)?**
   - **1:** Start from scratch or copy-paste from old files, heavy manual editing
   - **3:** We have templates, but merging client data is still manual
   - **5:** Template library with automated data population from case records

3. **How do you track deadlines and court dates?**
   - **1:** Individual calendars, sticky notes, hoping nothing gets missed
   - **3:** Shared calendar, but manual entry and reminder setting
   - **5:** Integrated docketing with automated deadline calculation and multi-channel reminders

4. **What happens when a document needs review or signature?**
   - **1:** Print, physical routing, hope it doesn't get lost
   - **3:** Email attachments with "please review" (version chaos)
   - **5:** Workflow-based routing with e-signature, automatic archiving when complete

5. **How easy is it to find a document from a case 2 years ago?**
   - **1:** 15-30 minutes digging through old files or emails
   - **3:** 5-10 minutes if we remember the client name and approximate date
   - **5:** 30 seconds with case management search

### Practice-Area Examples

**Bankruptcy:**
- Schedules, plans, adversary proceedings — these are template-heavy document sets. If you're rebuilding them manually each time, you're wasting billable hours.
- Chapter 13 plans that could be auto-generated from means test data are instead being manually typed. That's 45 minutes per case, minimum.

**Criminal:**
- Motions to suppress, discovery requests, sentencing memos — how much of each one is unique vs boilerplate?
- If your associates are copying old motions and doing find-replace, you're paying $150/hr for data entry.

**Administrative Law:**
- Agency filings, comment letters, hearing prep — these follow patterns. Pattern-following work should not require attorney time.
- If you're manually tracking which agency, which regulation, which deadline for every case, you're one missed deadline away from a malpractice claim.

## Section 3: Billing & Collections

**Score this section 1-5 based on these criteria:**

### Assessment Questions

1. **How do you capture billable time?**
   - **1:** End-of-day memory reconstruction (losing 20-30% of time)
   - **3:** Manual time entries throughout the day, but lots of forgotten tasks
   - **5:** Integrated time tracking that captures activity automatically as you work

2. **How long from work completed to invoice sent?**
   - **1:** End of month invoicing, 30+ days after work was done
   - **3:** Weekly or bi-weekly invoicing, but manual invoice creation
   - **5:** Real-time or daily invoicing, fully automated generation and sending

3. **What's your invoice payment rate within 30 days?**
   - **1:** Under 50% (chasing payments is a second full-time job)
   - **3:** 50-70% (better than most, but still lots of follow-up)

4. **How do you handle trust account (IOLTA) compliance?**
   - **1:** Manual spreadsheets, hoping we're compliant during audits
   - **3:** Accounting software, but reconciliation is manual and time-consuming
   - **5:** Integrated trust accounting with automated compliance checks and reporting

5. **How much time per week does someone spend on billing and collections?**
   - **1:** 10+ hours (nearly a full person just chasing money)
   - **3:** 4-6 hours (manageable but still a significant drag)
   - **5:** Under 2 hours (mostly reviewing exceptions, system handles the rest)

### Why This Matters

Billing and collections is where cash flow lives or dies. I was talking with a criminal defense attorney last year who realized he was losing $80K annually just from unbilled time — work he did but never captured. Not work he didn't get paid for. Work he literally never invoiced because he forgot to log it.

The math is simple: If you're doing $1M in revenue and losing 20% of billable time to capture failure, that's $200K you worked for and never got paid. If your collection rate is 60% instead of 85%, that's another $250K left on the table.

This section alone can justify the entire cost of automation.

## Section 4: Client Communication

**Score this section 1-5 based on these criteria:**

### Assessment Questions

1. **How do clients get case status updates?**
   - **1:** They call and ask "where's my case?" (reactive only)
   - **3:** We send updates when something happens, but it's manual and inconsistent
   - **5:** Automated status updates at key milestones, plus client portal for 24/7 access

2. **How many "where's my case?" calls do you get per week?**
   - **1:** 20+ calls (clients are anxious because they're in the dark)
   - **3:** 5-10 calls (better, but still interrupting billable work)
   - **5:** Under 5 (clients are informed proactively, rarely need to ask)

3. **How do you notify clients about court dates and deadlines?**
   - **1:** Phone calls or hoping the client remembers from the last conversation
   - **3:** Email or text a few days before, but manual sends
   - **5:** Automated multi-channel reminders (email, SMS) at 7 days, 3 days, 1 day out

4. **Do clients have self-service access to their case information?**
   - **1:** No, they have to call or email and wait for us to respond
   - **3:** We email documents when requested, but no centralized access
   - **5:** Client portal where they can see documents, deadlines, messages, invoices 24/7

5. **How do you handle routine client questions (payment plans, next steps, document requests)?**
   - **1:** Every question becomes a phone call or email that requires attorney/staff time
   - **3:** We have FAQs on the website, but clients still call with the same questions
   - **5:** Automated responses for common questions, portal for self-service, escalation only when truly needed

### The Hidden Cost of Communication

Client communication isn't just about client satisfaction — though that matters. It's about protecting your billable time.

Every "where's my case?" call is 10-15 minutes of your day (or your paralegal's day) that you can't bill for. If you're getting 20 of these per week, that's 3-4 hours of lost productivity. Annually, that's $25K-$40K in opportunity cost.

Bankruptcy clients are anxious because their financial life is in chaos. Criminal clients are anxious because their freedom is at stake. Administrative law clients are anxious because they're fighting bureaucracies. Anxiety creates phone calls. Proactive communication prevents phone calls.

The firms that get this right don't field fewer questions because they have better clients — they field fewer questions because their clients aren't in the dark.

## What "Automated" Actually Looks Like

This isn't theory. Here's what happens in a fully automated law firm when a new client makes contact:

**11:47 PM on a Tuesday:**
A potential bankruptcy client finds your website through a Google search. They're stressed, can't sleep, and they fill out your intake form.

**By 7:00 AM Wednesday:**
- Conflict check completed automatically (cross-referenced against existing and past client database)
- Client data validated and case file created in your case management system
- Engagement letter generated and sent via e-signature
- Payment link sent for initial retainer
- Automated email sent confirming receipt and outlining next steps
- Task created in your system for paralegal to review and make initial client contact

**By 9:00 AM Wednesday:**
- Client has signed engagement letter and paid retainer (they did this at 6:30 AM from their phone)
- Payment posted to trust account with IOLTA compliance logging
- Paralegal receives notification that case is ready for first contact
- Calendar hold created for initial client meeting
- Practice-specific intake form sent (bankruptcy means test, creditor list, asset schedule)

**No one on your team touched any of this until 9:00 AM.**

That client went from "I need help" to "I'm officially a client with a case file and an upcoming meeting" in under 12 hours, most of it while you were asleep.

Compare that to the typical process:
- Client calls during business hours, leaves voicemail
- Someone calls back (maybe same day, maybe next day)
- Phone tag for 2-3 days trying to schedule intake call
- Intake call happens, information collected manually
- Manual conflict check (hope you remember to do it)
- Engagement letter drafted and emailed
- Wait for signed letter to come back
- Follow up on payment
- Finally start the actual case

The automated version isn't just faster — it's more reliable, more compliant, and frees your team to do actual legal work instead of administrative coordination.

This isn't science fiction. This is what operations look like when you build systems instead of just buying software.
