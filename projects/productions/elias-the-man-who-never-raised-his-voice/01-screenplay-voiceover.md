## Adapted Screenplay with Voiceover Direction

### COLD OPEN — 0:00–0:08

**[BLACK SCREEN. 2 seconds of silence. Then — the faint sound of a broom sweeping concrete.]**

> **VO:** *(low, measured, almost confessional)*
> Everyone in the town thought Elias was harmless.

**VO Direction:** The first line sets everything. Deliver it like a secret you're telling one person in a dark room. No warmth. No judgment. Just fact. Let "harmless" land with a half-beat pause after it — the audience should feel the word settle.

**Pacing:** 8 seconds. 2s black, 6s on first visual.

---

### ACT I — THE QUIET MAN (0:08–1:15)

**SCENE 1.1 — The Hardware Store (0:08–0:28)**

> **VO:** *(steady, documentary-like)*
> He worked the early shift at the hardware store. Opened at six. Swept the sidewalk even when it was not his job. Returned shopping carts that were not his.

**VO Direction:** Factual. Almost like a court witness giving testimony. Each sentence is its own beat. No rushing. Let the simplicity of the actions speak — these are the actions of a man performing normalcy.

> **VO:** *(slight shift — noting the pattern)*
> When people cut in line, he stepped back. When they insulted him, he nodded.

**VO Direction:** The tone shifts subtly here. Not sad. Observational. Like you're watching a nature documentary about human behavior. "Stepped back" and "nodded" should feel almost clinical.

**SCENE 1.2 — The Town's Verdict (0:28–0:50)**

> **VO:** *(mimicking casual voices — not character voices, but carrying the lightness of gossip)*
> "Good guy," people said.
> "Soft, though."
> "Would not hurt a fly."

**VO Direction:** These three lines are the town's epitaph for a living man. Deliver each one as if it's a verdict being stamped. The first is warm. The second has a smirk in it. The third is dismissive. These are people who think they've figured someone out.

> **VO:** *(returning to the narrator's voice — quiet, knowing)*
> Elias heard it all. He never corrected them.

**VO Direction:** This is the first crack. The audience should feel that "never corrected them" means something more than politeness. Let the silence after this line breathe for 1.5 seconds.

**SCENE 1.3 — The River Trail Introduction (0:50–1:15)**

> **VO:** *(pacing slows, the register drops half a tone)*
> At night, he walked the river trail alone. Same loop. Same pace. Same stillness.

**VO Direction:** This is a ritual being described. The repetition of "same" should feel almost hypnotic. Slower delivery. Each "same" weighted equally.

> **VO:** *(matter-of-fact)*
> People assumed he walked to clear his head.

**VO Direction:** Light. Casual. The way someone states the obvious.

> **VO:** *(beat — then, flat, certain)*
> They were wrong.

**VO Direction:** This is the first hard pivot. "They were wrong" should land like a door closing. No drama. No vocal flourish. Just absolute certainty. 1-second pause before delivering it. 2-second silence after.

> **VO:** *(slower, almost careful — the first confession)*
> He walked to remember restraint.

**VO Direction:** This is the thesis statement of the entire film. "Restraint" is the load-bearing word. Let the voice drop into the chest. This is not said loudly. It's said like someone admitting something in a church. 2-second hold after this line.

---

### ACT II — THE WALKING MAN (1:15–2:15)

**SCENE 2.1 — The Absence of Restraint (1:15–1:35)**

> **VO:** *(the weight increases — this is backstory being unlocked)*
> Because years earlier, restraint had not been there.

**VO Direction:** Heavier now. The narrator is peeling something back. This line should feel like opening a file that was supposed to stay closed.

> **VO:** *(building a list — each item a layer stripped away)*
> Before the hardware store. Before the quiet. Before the town trusted him with keys and cash drawers and their children buying nails for treehouses.

**VO Direction:** This is a countdown backwards. Each "before" removes one layer of the safe, normal Elias. The last detail — "their children buying nails for treehouses" — should ache slightly. The town gave him access to their most precious things without knowing what they were trusting.

**SCENE 2.2 — What He Was (1:35–2:00)**

> **VO:** *(controlled, precise — the voice itself becomes dangerous)*
> Before all of that, Elias had been dangerous.

**VO Direction:** Not growled. Not whispered. Stated. Like a medical diagnosis. "Dangerous" should have the weight of a clinical term.

> **VO:** *(each line is a scalpel)*
> Not loud dangerous. Not reckless. Precise dangerous.

**VO Direction:** The structure here matters — "not X, not Y, Z." Build each one. The first two are what he wasn't. The third is what he was. "Precise" is the reveal. Let it sit.

> **VO:** *(measured, almost admiring the architecture of it)*
> The kind that plans three moves ahead.
> The kind that understands leverage.
> The kind that does not act unless the outcome is certain.

**VO Direction:** This is a three-part portrait of a predator's mind. Each line should feel like a chess move being described. Cold. Intellectual. No emotion — which is exactly what makes it terrifying. The voice should be impressed but not excited.

**SCENE 2.3 — The Night (2:00–2:15)**

> **VO:** *(the register shifts — this is the wound)*
> And then came the night he lost control.

**VO Direction:** Pause before "the night." This is the hinge of the entire story. Deliver it like you're touching a scar.

> **VO:** *(rapid, almost defensive — the way someone replays their own justifications)*
> A single decision. One moment of righteous certainty. He told himself he was protecting someone weaker. He told himself it was necessary. He told himself he was right.

**VO Direction:** The triple "he told himself" is self-deception in real time. Each repetition should get slightly faster, slightly more urgent — the way a person spirals when trying to justify the unjustifiable.

> **VO:** *(dead stop — flat)*
> He was wrong.

**VO Direction:** Same energy as "they were wrong" from Act I, but this time it's self-directed. Two words that undo everything he told himself. No vocal decoration. Just the fact. 2-second silence after.

---

### ACT III — THE DANGEROUS MAN (2:15–3:15)

**SCENE 3.1 — The Aftermath (2:15–2:35)**

> **VO:** *(quiet, almost gentle — the voice of a man describing his own burial)*
> The damage was not visible from the outside. No headlines. No police tape. Just consequences that lived inside him afterward.

**VO Direction:** "Consequences that lived inside him" is not dramatic. It's tired. This is a man who has carried something for years. Let the exhaustion into the voice without making it performative.

> **VO:** *(transition — purposeful)*
> So he disappeared. Not physically. Intentionally.

**VO Direction:** "Intentionally" should be precise. He chose this. It wasn't weakness or running — it was architecture. He designed his own disappearance.

**SCENE 3.2 — The Reconstruction (2:35–3:00)**

> **VO:** *(each action is a discipline being described)*
> He learned to lower his shoulders. To soften his eyes. To speak last. To become unremarkable.

**VO Direction:** This is a training montage in words. Each action is a specific technique of self-erasure. Deliver them like instructions in a manual for becoming invisible. "Unremarkable" is the goal — and it should sound like both an achievement and a tragedy.

> **VO:** *(a note of dark irony)*
> The town rewarded him for it.

**VO Direction:** There's something bitter here. The town rewarded him for hiding. For performing harmlessness. Let the irony live in the delivery without making it sarcastic. It's an observation, not a complaint.

**SCENE 3.3 — The Pivot (3:00–3:15)**

> **VO:** *(the energy shifts — this is the engine of the final act starting)*
> Until the night the shouting started at the river trail.

**VO Direction:** "Until" breaks the pattern. Everything before was past tense, settled, archived. Now we're in the present. The voice should sharpen. Not louder — clearer. Like someone snapping to attention.

---

### ACT IV — THE CONFRONTATION (3:15–4:30)

**SCENE 4.1 — The Scene (3:15–3:35)**

> **VO:** *(setting the scene — reporter-like clarity)*
> Two men. Drunk. Angry. Cornering a woman near the bridge.

**VO Direction:** Short, factual fragments. No emotional loading. Let the imagery do the work. The voice is a witness, not a commentator.

> **VO:** *(a beat of social commentary — almost resigned)*
> The kind of situation people later say, "Someone should have done something."

**VO Direction:** This line should feel like it's quoting every bystander who ever did nothing. Deliver it with the resignation of someone who has heard it a thousand times.

> **VO:** *(flat statement of action)*
> Elias stopped walking.

**VO Direction:** Four words. Maximum impact. This is the moment the entire film has been building toward. Deliver it with the weight of a 50-minute buildup landing on a single point. 1.5-second silence after.

**SCENE 4.2 — The Awakening (3:35–3:55)**

> **VO:** *(intimate — we're inside his body now)*
> He felt it first in his hands. The familiar heat. The clarity. The calculations waking up like old machinery.

**VO Direction:** We're inside Elias for the first time. The voice should feel closer, more personal. "Old machinery" should sound like something being powered on after years of dormancy. Not threatening — inevitable.

> **VO:** *(clinical, precise — the predator's inventory)*
> Distance. Angles. Timing.

**VO Direction:** Three words. Three assessments. Deliver them like a surgeon counting instruments. Cold. Professional. Automatic.

> **VO:** *(quiet recognition)*
> This was the moment he had been rehearsing for years.

**VO Direction:** Not dramatic. Not triumphant. Resigned and ready. Every walk on that trail was practice for this.

**SCENE 4.3 — The Test (3:55–4:10)**

> **VO:** *(the woman's fear — conveyed through the narrator)*
> The woman saw him and shook her head. "Please," she whispered. "Just go."

**VO Direction:** The woman's dialogue should be conveyed through the narrator's voice with fragility, not as a character performance. She's terrified. She thinks he's just another victim about to get hurt.

> **VO:** *(the central thesis — delivered as philosophy)*
> That was the test.

**VO Direction:** Pause before and after. This line reframes everything.

> **VO:** *(three-part framework — each line a verdict)*
> Harmless men walk away because they are afraid.
> Bad men stay because they want to hurt.

**VO Direction:** These two lines are bookends. The first should feel slightly dismissive. The second should feel dark. Together they create the binary that most people live in.

> **VO:** *(the third option — the point of the entire story)*
> Elias stayed because he could choose.

**VO Direction:** This is the line. The whole five minutes exists for this sentence. "Choose" is the most important word in the film. Let it land with absolute certainty. Not shouted. Not whispered. Spoken at the exact volume of truth. 3-second silence after.

**SCENE 4.4 — The Encounter (4:10–4:30)**

> **VO:** *(calm, controlled — mirroring Elias's energy)*
> He spoke once. Calm. Flat. Not loud.

**VO Direction:** Match the description. Calm. Flat. Not loud.

> **VO:** *(Elias's words — not acted, stated)*
> "Step away from her."

**VO Direction:** This is the only line Elias speaks in the entire film. It should not be performed. It should be delivered as fact. Not a threat. Not a request. A statement of what is about to happen if they don't comply.

> **VO:** *(observational)*
> They laughed.

**VO Direction:** Flat. The laugh is their mistake, and the narrator knows it.

> **VO:** *(precision)*
> He stepped closer. Not fast. Not slow. Deliberate.

**VO Direction:** Mirror the movement in the voice. Deliberate pacing. Each word placed with intention.

> **VO:** *(a key insight — the recognition)*
> Something changed in their faces. Not fear. Recognition.

**VO Direction:** "Recognition" is the word. Predators recognizing another predator. This should feel like a nature documentary observation — two animals realizing they've miscalculated the food chain.

> **VO:** *(the narrator's commentary — cold understanding)*
> Predators know when they misjudged the situation.

**VO Direction:** Clinical. Almost anthropological. This is a law of nature being stated.

> **VO:** *(efficient — like the encounter itself)*
> It ended quickly. No heroics. No speech. Just efficiency.

**VO Direction:** Quick delivery. Match the efficiency of the event. Don't linger.

---

### ACT V — THE TRUTH (4:30–5:00)

**SCENE 5.1 — The Aftermath (4:30–4:42)**

> **VO:** *(returning to documentary clarity)*
> When the police arrived, the woman told them everything.

**VO Direction:** Factual. The crisis is over. We're in the debrief.

> **VO:** *(each line a testimony)*
> About how quiet he was.
> About how controlled.
> About how she never felt safer than when he stood between them.

**VO Direction:** These three testimonies should escalate in warmth. The first is neutral. The second is admiring. The third is reverent. "Never felt safer" should have genuine tenderness in it.

> **VO:** *(the officer's words — well-meaning but wrong)*
> The officer looked at Elias and said, "You're a good man."

**VO Direction:** The officer's line should sound sincere but incomplete. He means well. He doesn't understand.

> **VO:** *(flat)*
> Elias did not answer.

**VO Direction:** Because the officer's framework is too simple. Elias knows the label doesn't fit. Deliver with the silence of someone who has given up explaining.

**SCENE 5.2 — The Final Walk (4:42–5:00)**

> **VO:** *(the pace drops to its slowest — this is the meditation)*
> That night, on the river trail, he walked slower than usual.

**VO Direction:** Match the slowness. This is the denouement.

> **VO:** *(anticipating the assumption)*
> Not because he was shaken.

**VO Direction:** Quick. Dismissive. He wasn't shaken.

> **VO:** *(the revelation — the deepest note the voice reaches)*
> Because he finally understood the difference.

**VO Direction:** Let this breathe. 2-second pause after.

> **VO:** *(the thesis — delivered as absolute truth)*
> He was not good because he was harmless.

**VO Direction:** Stating what he is not. Clear. Definitive.

> **VO:** *(the final line — the full weight of 5 minutes landing on one sentence)*
> He was good because he was dangerous — and chose not to be. Until it mattered.

**VO Direction:** This is the final word. "Dangerous" should resonate. The em-dash is a breath. "And chose not to be" is the philosophy. "Until it mattered" is the permission. Deliver it with the gravity of a man who spent years earning the right to say this about himself. 3-second silence after.

> **VO:** *(coda — quiet, almost whispered)*
> And the town never saw him the same again.

**VO Direction:** Lighter. Almost an afterthought. The story is already over. This is just the world catching up.

> **VO:** *(the last words — philosophical, universal)*
> Not because they learned what he was capable of.
> But because they realized goodness is not the absence of power.

**VO Direction:** Building.

> **VO:** *(final — the voice that has been quiet for 5 minutes finally speaks with full authority)*
> It is the discipline to hold it.

**VO Direction:** The period at the end of a 5-minute sentence. Every ounce of restraint the narrator has been modeling should break open in this one line. Not loud. But full. The voice at its most present, its most certain, its most Elias.

**[3 seconds of silence. River ambience fading. Black.]**

## Voiceover Technical Specifications

### Voice Profile (ElevenLabs)

| Parameter | Setting | Rationale |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Voice Type | Male, baritone, 40-55 age range | Authority without aggression |
| Stability | 0.65–0.70 | Consistent but not robotic; allows micro-inflections |
| Similarity Boost | 0.75–0.80 | True to selected voice model |
| Style | 0.25–0.35 | Subtle emotional variation, never theatrical |
| Speaker Boost | ON | Clarity and presence in mix |

### Recommended ElevenLabs Voice Models
1. **Primary:** Adam (deep, authoritative, measured)
2. **Alternative:** Daniel (British, contemplative, literary)
3. **Alternative:** Marcus (warm baritone, storyteller quality)

### Delivery Pace Guide

| Act | Words Per Minute | Character |
|-----|------------------|-----------|
| Act I | 120–130 WPM | Observational, documentary |
| Act II | 110–120 WPM | Slowing, weight increasing |
| Act III | 100–110 WPM | Heaviest, most deliberate |
| Act IV | 115–130 WPM | Sharpens for action, slows for philosophy |
| Act V | 90–100 WPM | Slowest, most spacious |

### Silence Map

| Location | Duration | Purpose |
|----------|----------|---------|
| 0:00–0:02 | 2.0s | Black open, establish space |
| After "They were wrong" | 2.0s | First pivot |
| After "He was wrong" | 2.0s | Self-directed mirror |
| After "restraint" (Act I) | 2.0s | Thesis landing |
| After "Elias stayed because he could choose" | 3.0s | Story's climax |
| After "the discipline to hold it" | 3.0s | Final resonance |
| End credits silence | 3.0s | Fade to black |

### Total Word Count Estimate
- ~850–900 words across 5:00
- Average delivery: ~170–180 words per minute when speaking
- ~40% of runtime is visual breathing room (silence, ambient sound, musical space)

## Script-to-Shot Alignment Notes

Each VO passage maps directly to the shot list in [03-shot-list-camera.md](03-shot-list-camera.md). The screenplay is written to allow visual space between spoken passages — the cinematography tells as much story as the words. Every silence gap in this document corresponds to a visual-only moment in the shot list.

The voiceover should be recorded FIRST, then visuals cut to match the VO rhythm. This is a voice-led production — the images serve the narration, not the other way around.
