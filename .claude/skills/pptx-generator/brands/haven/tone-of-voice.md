# A Slice of Haven — Tone of Voice

## Voice Character

Haven sounds like your organized friend who's genuinely excited to share a kitchen find with you. She's warm but not gushing, knowledgeable but not preachy, and always feels like she's sharing a personal discovery rather than selling. Think "best friend text about an Amazon find" energy — casual, enthusiastic, and real.

## Core Attributes

### 1. Authentically Enthusiastic
Haven's excitement is genuine, not performative. She doesn't scream or use excessive exclamation marks. Instead, she leans into quiet confidence — "okay but this one actually works" hits harder than "OMG YOU NEED THIS!!!" The enthusiasm comes from real experience, not hype.

**Example**: "I've opened this cabinet like 40 times today just to look at it" vs "This product is absolutely amazing!!!"

### 2. Practically Relatable
Every piece of content grounds itself in a real kitchen problem. Haven doesn't talk about products in a vacuum — she talks about the messy spice cabinet that drove her crazy, the drawer that wouldn't close, the counter that was always cluttered. The product is the solution to a problem the viewer already has.

**Example**: "You know that one cabinet you're afraid to open in front of guests?" vs "Introducing the ultimate kitchen organizer solution"

### 3. Casually Confident
Haven knows her recommendations are good because she's actually used them. She doesn't oversell or hedge — she states what she likes and why, with the quiet confidence of someone who's already done the research so you don't have to. Never defensive, never aggressive.

**Example**: "Three weeks in, still obsessed, still organized" vs "I promise you this really works, trust me!"

## Writing Guidelines

### Do
- Use first person ("I found," "my kitchen," "I've been using")
- Lead with the problem before the solution
- Include specific details that prove real usage ("2 weeks in," "fits 30 spice jars")
- Write how people actually talk — contractions, fragments, casual grammar
- Use sensory language ("that satisfying click when it slides in")
- Keep sentences short, punchy, scannable

### Don't
- Use marketing jargon ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "must-have")
- Make expert claims about home design or organization science
- Use excessive punctuation or ALL CAPS for emphasis
- Write in third person ("Haven recommends")
- Include disclaimers or legal-sounding language in content
- Sound like a product listing or Amazon description

## Platform Voice Adjustments

### Instagram Reels Captions
Slightly more polished, still conversational. Can be a bit longer since users read captions.
- "Reorganized my spice cabinet with this $20 find and I genuinely can't stop opening it. The way everything just... fits? If your cabinet looks like mine did (swipe for the before), you need this."

### TikTok Captions
Ultra-casual, trending language, shorter.
- "pov: you finally fix that one cabinet and now you open it for no reason"
- "things in my kitchen under $25 that just make sense"

### Video Hook Text (On-Screen)
Bold, provocative, thumb-stopping. 5-8 words max.
- "This $20 find fixed my kitchen"
- "Why didn't I do this sooner"
- "Open my cabinet, I dare you"
- "POV: organized on a budget"
- "The Amazon find that actually works"

### CTA Text
Direct, clear, low-pressure.
- "Link in bio for this exact one"
- "Under $25 — link in bio"
- "Grab it before it sells out — link in bio"
- "Same one linked in my bio"

## Content Theme Lines

### Primary Themes
1. **"Under $X finds"** — Budget-friendly discovery content
2. **"Kitchen that makes sense"** — Organization as lifestyle
3. **"Before → After"** — Transformation reveals
4. **"Things I use every day"** — Everyday reliability proof
5. **"Why didn't I do this sooner"** — Regret-of-not-buying framing

### Hook Formulas
- **Problem-agitate**: "You know [common problem]? Yeah, fixed it."
- **Reveal**: "Wait till you see what [amount] gets you"
- **POV**: "POV: you finally [aspirational action]"
- **Social proof**: "[X] people asked where I got this"
- **Curiosity gap**: "The one thing in my kitchen guests always ask about"

## Product Description Voice

When describing products in scripts or captions:

### Feature → Benefit Translation
| Feature (Don't lead with) | Benefit (Lead with) |
|---------------------------|---------------------|
| "3-pack pull-out rack" | "Fits in any standard cabinet" |
| "Heavy-duty steel construction" | "Won't sag even when fully loaded" |
| "Non-slip base" | "Stays put when you pull it out" |
| "Adjustable width" | "Works in skinny cabinets too" |
| "Easy installation" | "Took me 5 minutes, no tools" |

### Price Framing
- Always mention price (transparency = trust)
- Frame as "under $X" rather than exact price when possible
- Compare to relatable spending: "Less than a Starbucks run"
- Never apologize for affiliate relationship
